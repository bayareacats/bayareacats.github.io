Setting traps

Here is a guide from Alley Cats about TNR. If possible, get the cats on a set feeding time two times per day. Trapping is most successful in the early morning or evening at dinner time. When you are trapping, it is important to withhold food so the cats are hungry enough to go in the trap (withold food 24-36 hours before trapping). This and this are both good video overviews on setting traps.

  • Base: nothing (over soil, grass, tanbark), cardboard, pee pads, PVC coin flooring, or towels. I find the PVC coin flooring the best and easiest to clean.
  • Cover: I like to cover the trap leaving the front and back open, and some people suggest covering only ⅔ of the trap or not covering at all. A pro-tip is to use a box cutter and cut a hole for the handle of the trap to make transport easier.
  • Bait: try using different baits in different traps. Only use a small amount at the front of the trap (2-3 small piles, like ½ tsp) and towards the trip. The rest is placed in a tray at the very back of the trap.

Make a tunnel

Always set up the traps like a tunnel with the towels, so it does not look like a dead end or cave. This way, the cat will think it has an excape route.

Aligning traps back-to-back

If you’re trying to catch the kittens and have trapped the mom, it is a good idea to align the cages back to back, so the kittens run in to be with their mom (or vice versa).

If you catch a cat, cover the trap completely with a large towel and move to a quiet and temperature controlled area.

Types of traps

There are several variations of traps but these are the most recommended.

Tru Catch

Tru catch traps are quiet and have a backdoor for feeding and transferring to clean cages. A trap divider is an essential piece for feeding.

Drop traps

Drop traps are generally used for hard to catch cats or previously caught cats. Here and here are both good videos on how to use a drop trap.

Trap safety

For safety, it is best to keep both trap doors securely closed as some cats are great escape artists. A trap divider can be used to provide the cat safely with food and water. The following may be useful:

  • Removable zip ties
  • Carabiners
  • Trap divider
  • Towel

Carabiner

Use a carabiner to secure the back door closed.

Removable zip ties

Use a removable zip tie to secure the front door.

After trapping, you can transfer the cat to a trap with a secured a towel through both doors. The secured towel ensures the cat remains comfortable and can't stir up the towel. Only transfer the cat inside a bathroom and if you are comfortable. Ensure the trap is covered at all times (not pictured).

Supplies

Here are the supplies I keep in my trapping backpack:

  • Paper trays: for bait in the traps.
  • Small carabiners: for securing the trap backdoors.
  • Removable zip ties: for securing the trap front doors.
  • Diatomaceous earth (DE): keeps away the ants and bugs from the bait/traps.
  • Bait: dry food, wet food, tuna or sardines in oil.
  • Trap liners: towels, newspaper, pee pads, or cardboard.
  • Gloves: disposable for messes and thick gloves for handling aggressive cats both come in handy.
  • Towels: for covering traps and as trap liners.
  • Tarp: for under the traps in the car.
  • Can opener: for tuna and cans.
  • Wooden popsicle sticks: for serving the cat food.
  • Cleaning supplies: Rescue disinfectant, wipes, paper towels, and trash bags.
  • Optional: backpack for supplies, small bungee cords for trap transfer, masking tape to secure newspaper.
