Kitten care

I am not a kitten expert, but this is what I did for the little ones I found: Immediately give them kitten-safe flea meds when you get them home and a bath within 24-48 hours if they are healthy (kitten shampoo, kitten wipes). They should see a vet within the first few days, if not the day of capture. Find a low-cost vet on the Resources page. Keep your own pets safe by keeping the kittens separated from other pets and wash your hands and change your clothes after interacting with them.

Feeding

Kittens 4-5 weeks old will need extra care and might need some kitten milk formula depending on size. Ask your vet how often to feed them. When they are 5-7 weeks old, they can have 50% diluted kitten food with water. This is just guidance. Everything should be confirmed with your vet. Good quality wet food brands include Nulo and Weruva. In wet food, kitten vs cat food is not that important. When kittens are 8 weeks old or so, they can be transitioned to solid kitten food.

Litter box

Depending on the size of the kitten, you can use a shoebox lid for their litter box. Otherwise use a small, kitten sized litter box. If they are struggling to use the litter box, decrease how much space they have and take away floor towels. You can also place them in the litterbox a few times a day. World’s Best and Arm & Hammer both are good litters.

Supplements

A volunteer at Itty Bitty Orphan Kitty rescue gave me some colostrum (1/3 capsule per feeding) and some probiotics (small pinch - ⅛ teaspoon per feeding for 3 kittens) to help their digestive system. Consult a vet before using.

Socialization

Once they are healthy enough, they should be socialized as much as possible. Examples: Expose them to vacuum cleaner sounds, trash bags, loud yelling, closing cabinets, strange noises, as many new people as possible, kids, other cat-friendly dogs or cats, both men and women, and anything else you can think of. Please be very careful and move slowly when introducing your kittens to other pets (always have a safety barrier).

Don’t give them too much freedom too soon, keep them in a bathroom or other small space. Make sure any room they enter doesn’t have any hiding spaces. You do not want them to learn to “dart” under furniture when they are afraid.

Never let them play-bite, paw, or scratch at you, your hair or your fingers. This is a very bad habit for adult cats. Any time they try to bite you, substitute your hand with a toy. Soft toys with a noise inside are really good for this. Do not ever yell at or scold the kitten.

Vaccinations & Spay/Neuter

Kittens can be spayed or neutered once they reach 3 lbs. When they get fixed, they need to get a microchip. Low cost vaccination clinics can be found on the Resources page. This is a rough outline; check with your vet about what is best for your kittens.

  • Flea treatment
