Kitten care

Rescuing kittens • Vaccinations • Supplies

I am not a kitten expert, but this is a compilation of advice and recommendations of cat people and cat rescues.

Rescuing Kittens

Immediately give them kitten-safe flea meds when you get them home and a bath within 24-48 hours if they are healthy (kitten shampoo, kitten wipes). They should see a vet within the first few days, if not the day of capture. Find a low-cost vet on the Resources page. Keep your own pets safe by keeping the kittens separated from other pets and wash your hands and change your clothes after interacting with them.

Feeding. Kittens 4-5 weeks old will need extra care and might need some kitten milk formula depending on size. Ask your vet how often to feed them. When they are 5-7 weeks old, they can have wet food with a bit of warm water. Good quality wet food brands include Nulo and Weruva. When kittens are 8 weeks old or so, they can be transitioned to solid kitten food such as Blue Buffalo.

Litter box. Depending on the size of the kitten, you can use a shoebox lid for their litter box. Otherwise use a small, kitten sized litter box. If they are struggling to use the litter box, decrease how much space they have and take away any extra blankets or towels. You can also place them in the litterbox a few times a day. World’s Best and Arm & Hammer both are good litters to start.

Supplements. A volunteer at Itty Bitty Orphan Kitty rescue gave me some colostrum (1/3 capsule per feeding) and some probiotics (small pinch - ⅛ teaspoon per feeding for 3 kittens) to help their digestive system. Consult a vet before using.

Socialization. Expose them to vacuum cleaner sounds, trash bags, loud yelling, closing cabinets, strange noises, as many new people as possible, kids, other cat-friendly dogs or cats, both men and women, and anything else you can think of. Don’t give them too much freedom too soon, keep them in a bathroom or other small space so they don't have any hiding spaces (e.g. under the couch) or develop a bad habit to run. Never let them play-bite, paw, or scratch at you, your hair or your fingers. This is a very bad habit for adult cats. Any time they try to bite you, substitute your hand with a toy (preferably stuffed).

Vaccinations & Spay/Neuter

Kittens can be spayed or neutered once they reach 3 lbs. When they get fixed, they need to get a microchip. Low cost vaccination clinics can be found on the Resources page. This is a rough outline; check with your vet about what is best for your kittens.

8 weeks

  • FVRCP#1
  • Flea treatment

12 weeks

  • FVRCP#2
  • FeLV#1
  • Flea treatment

16 weeks

  • FVRCP#3
  • FeLV#2
  • Rabies
  • Flea treatment

Supplies

Here is a list of supplies you might consider before bringing home your new kitten.

Litter box

Two good litterbox options include:

  • Modkat top-entry with walnut, clay, or wood litter.
  • Sifting litter box with pine pellets which turn to sawdust (video)

  • Modkat top-entry

  • Sifting box

  • Litter Genie

Food

Wet food is good for kittens as they don't remember to drink, and cat vs kitten food is only important in dry food. You'll want to get three bowls (wet food, dry food, water).


  • Nulo (wet)

  • Small bowls

  • Blue Buffalo (dry)

Health

It's good to get the kittens used to being brushed and having nail trims. Cat wipes are also good to have on hand.


  • Brush

  • Flea medicine

  • Nail clippers

Other

Instead of containing the kitten in a bathroom, a play pen works very well to contain them in a smaller area, and to encourage good litter box habits.


  • Play pen

  • Cat tree

  • Washable bed

  • Carrier

Toys

Kittens love to play, but need a variety of toys to stay entertained. A stuffed toy is good to substitute for when they try to bite/paw at your hands. Here are some suggestions.


  • Wands

  • Balls

  • Stuffed toys

  • Spring toys

  • Turbo scratcher

  • Tower toy

  • Cat teaser

  • Laser toy
